Quicksort

Quicksort is a comparison sort algorithm in the divide and conquer style. It will quickly sort large collections, on average taking O(n log n) to do it.

It will sort an array "in-place", meaning it does not need to copy elements into other arrays, and taking up more space. Quicksort can be implemented both recursively and iteratively, but the aforementioned is likely the more common type, and the one used on this page.

Comparisons in a section of the array are made against a pivot point. This can be chosen randomly, but the middle element is good idea and used here. The pivot can be seen in the animation above as the green bar. After the pivot is chosen two pointers will iterate through the array from both sides of the pivot. These are seen in the animation above as the blue bars for the left side, and red bars for the right side.

Iteration continues until the index of the left pointer is no longer smaller than the index of the right pointer. During the iteration, the poiners will stop moving when the element at their index is larger (for the left pointer) or smaller (for the right pointer) than the pivot element. When both pointers have stopped moving, it means they have found two elements that should be swapped.

When this so-called "partitioning" is completed a new pointer is returned that represents either the most left or most right side of a new section of the array that should be sorted. Quicksort is called again from within its function. This new section to be searched can with quick eyes be identified through it starting with a blue bar on the left side, ending on a bar, and with a green bar (the pivot) in or near the middle.

Once the partition function returns an index that is larger than both the left side and the right side the Quicksort function no longer calls new Quicksorts and returns. When all the called Quicksorts have returned the sorting is over.
